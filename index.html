I think of myself as a hybrid — part physicist, part engineer, and somewhere between an experimentalist and a theorist. I like that I don't have to choose.

My work sits at the intersection of topological photonics, nonlinear optics, and frequency combs — using the geometry of photonic lattices to make optical "rulers" that are stable, broadband, and chip-scale. The dream: take Nobel-Prize-worthy physics out of the textbook and turn it into something useful in the real world.

Fundamental science is cool. But I really want it to do something — that's the part that gets me out of bed.

— Lida

When I'm not in the lab

Things that also matter to me.

The people I work with

I've been lucky to work with brilliant collaborators across UMD, NIST, and beyond. Science is a team sport, and the team makes it worth doing.

🌉

Bridging worlds

Theory and experiment, physics and engineering, fundamental and applied — I like the messy in-between. Sharp boundaries are usually wrong.

🛠️

Building tools

I write a lot of simulation software for our group, much of it open source. There's something satisfying about a tool that other people actually use.

📚

Translating science

I like explaining hard ideas in simple language. If a Nobel-Prize idea can't be told to a curious friend, we haven't finished understanding it yet.

I'm always up for a good conversation — about photonics, collaborations, or anything else.
