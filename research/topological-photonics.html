Research direction · 01

Topological photonics.

Building optical chips whose physics is dictated by geometry, so light flows along boundaries that disorder can't disrupt.

The discoveries of the integer quantum Hall effect (1985 Nobel), the fractional quantum Hall effect (1998 Nobel), and the development of the modern framework of topological phases (2016 Nobel) revealed that topology is not merely a mathematical abstraction, but a fundamental organizing principle of quantum matter. These phases exhibit hallmark properties such as quantized conductance and robust boundary modes that remain protected against disorder and imperfections.

By translating these concepts into photonics, we design optical structures whose bands inherit the same topological invariants that stabilize electronic edge states.

As a result, these systems support topologically protected optical edge modes that guide light along boundaries without backscattering, even in the presence of fabrication imperfections. This robustness enables disorder-immune routing, synthetic gauge fields for photons, and exploration of new regimes in non-Hermitian and nonlinear topological physics.

Why I find this compelling

The promise of topological photonics is that the geometry of a chip can do work that previously required exquisite fabrication tolerances. If a device works because of its shape rather than the perfection of its parts, then we can build photonic systems at scale — and that's the door this field opens.
