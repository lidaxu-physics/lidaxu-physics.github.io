Research direction · 02

Integrated nonlinear photonics.

Bringing the precision of optical frequency combs out of laser labs and onto chips small enough to integrate into real systems.

Optical frequency combs revolutionized precision measurement — a breakthrough recognized by the 2005 Nobel — by providing an exquisitely stable ruler for measuring optical frequencies with unprecedented accuracy. Traditional combs, however, rely on large, complex femtosecond laser systems. In the past decade, a major scientific push has aimed to miniaturize this Nobel-Prize–winning capability onto chip-scale platforms.

Microresonator frequency combs, or microcombs, achieve this by confining continuous-wave laser light inside a high-Q cavity, where intense circulating fields drive Kerr nonlinearities and generate a series of equally spaced spectral lines. These chip-based combs can operate at microwave repetition rates, produce coherent solitons, and integrate directly with photonic circuits.

Their compactness, stability, and CMOS compatibility position microcombs as powerful tools for next-generation precision metrology, telecommunications, and emerging nonlinear and quantum technologies.

What I work on here

I focus on the integrated side — making nonlinear photonic devices that are not just clever physics demonstrations, but reliable, reproducible, and ready to be built into real systems. The hard problem isn't getting any one comb to work; it's getting the same comb to work twice, on hundreds of chips fabricated in different runs.

That's where my work on multi-timescale frequency-phase matching comes in. By engineering the matching condition across multiple timescales simultaneously, we can stabilize the comb generation process against the small fabrication variations that have plagued integrated nonlinear optics for years.
